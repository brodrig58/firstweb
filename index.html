Mario Molina

Born: March 9th, 1943
Primarily worked for: Institute Professor at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology
University: Freiburg

Biography

Mario Molina was a Mexican-born American chemist who was awarded the 1955 Nobel Prize for Chemistry. He received this award because of his research concering the decomposition of the ozonosphere, which shields Earth from dangerous solar radiation. Molina studied chemical engineering at the National Autonomous Universtiy of Mexico in Mexico City and received a degree from the University of Freiburg. Molina worked at the University of California, Berkley, for a year before joining Rowland at the Univeristy of California, Irvine. The 2 of them conducted experiments on pollutants in the atmosphere and more. Molina was the principal author of the paper describing their theories. Molina also worked in the Jet Propulsion Laboratory at the California Institute of Technology in Pasadena. Molina was awared the U.S. Presidential Medal of Freedom in 2013.

Notable Contributions:

  • Discovered that CFCs break down in sunlight and release chlorina gas, which harms the ozone layer.
  • Collaboraetd with colleagues on air-polution problems in growing cities.
  • Improved the air quality in Mexico City.

Famous Quote:

"Finding out for myself, for the first time, how something works is really an enormous driving force."
